Supported by Science

Eye contact matters. In a study by Tessler & Sushelsky (1978), eye contact demonstrated to interviewers an interviewee's genuine interest. There is a high correlation between amount of eye contact and a hiring decision, the single variable with the greatest relationship to interview scores (Aamodt, 1986).

A recent study by Gikas and Sutcliffe (2019) showed that filler words often contribute to a negative expectation violation and a lack of professionalism. Especially during interviews, it's important to maintain the highest standard of professionalism and avoid using fillers that can harm your credibility and perceived communication competence.

Vocal interview cues have an enormous impact on interviewers' judgements and correlates with supervisory ratings of job performance (DeGroot and Motowidlo, 1999). We're here to ensure your vocal patterns and volume don't prevent you from getting the job you want.
